# Mid-Block Defense Patterns

## Core Idea

Defend from the middle third with compact spacing and controlled pressure.

The mid-block does not chase the first pass. It protects the center, waits for predictable progression, then presses once the opponent enters a prepared zone.

## Base Structure

```text
      ST    ST
LM   CM   CM   RM
LB   CB   CB   RB
```

## Main Triggers

* pass into a full-back
* pass into a midfielder facing backward
* touch toward the sideline
* square pass with no forward body shape
* opponent center-back carrying beyond the first line

## Player Movements

* front line screens passes into the pivot
* midfield four narrows around the ball
* near winger presses when the ball goes wide
* full-back holds line until winger engages
* far winger tucks in to protect the switch lane

## Typical Pattern

```text
CB carries -> pass to RB
LM jumps to press
LB locks winger
LCM shifts across
ST blocks return into CB
```

Goal: deny central progression and force the opponent into wide or backward passes.

## Strengths

* balances protection and pressure
* reduces space between the lines
* preserves energy better than constant high pressing

## Risks

* can become passive if no one jumps
* vulnerable to switches if weak-side spacing is poor
* requires disciplined distances between midfield and defense

## Elite Usage

Common for teams that want control without overexposing the back line. Diego Simeone's Atletico Madrid and many elite knockout teams use mid-block principles to manage risk.
