# Hybrid Defending Patterns

## Core Idea

Combine zonal structure with man-oriented pressure near the ball.

Hybrid defending protects the team shape while still allowing aggressive jumps onto dangerous receivers.

## Base Structure

```text
Zonal block away from ball
Man-oriented locks near ball
Spare defender covers depth
```

## Main Triggers

* ball enters a prepared pressing zone
* opponent's playmaker receives between lines
* full-back receives near touchline
* striker drops into midfield
* team has cover behind the press

## Player Movements

* near-side players lock onto direct opponents
* far-side players remain zonal and narrow
* holding midfielder covers the space behind jumping midfielders
* center-back follows striker only if cover remains
* goalkeeper and back line manage depth

## Typical Pattern

```text
Ball to LB
RW presses LB
RCM locks LCM
RB locks LW
DM stays zonal inside
```

Goal: apply tight pressure without letting the whole team be dragged apart.

## Strengths

* flexible against rotations
* creates local pressure while preserving balance
* fits modern pressing and blocking systems

## Risks

* unclear responsibilities can create hesitation
* requires players to read when to follow and when to release
* poor cover turns local aggression into open space

## Elite Usage

Common in elite football because most top teams need both structured compactness and aggressive pressure around the ball.
