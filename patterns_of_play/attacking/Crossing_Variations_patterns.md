# Attack Form: Crossing Variations (Full Team Movement System)

Crossing is not one action—it is a **family of delivery patterns**, each with a different timing, trajectory, and supporting movement structure.

In elite attacking systems, crosses are not random:

> They are the final phase of a coordinated team structure that creates box occupation, timing runs, and second-ball control.

I’ll break down each crossing type with:

* build-up actions
* full 11-player movement
* box occupation patterns
* finishing logic

---

# BASE ATTACK STRUCTURE (REFERENCE)

```text id="cr0"
          ST
LW                    RW

     LCM     RCM
          DM

LB   LCB     RCB     RB
```

---

# 1) EARLY CROSS ATTACK (BEFORE DEFENSE SETS)

## Core idea:

Cross is delivered **before full defensive organization is complete**.

---

## Step 1: Trigger Phase

Ball is in wide zone.

```text id="cr1"
LW OR RW receives early in transition
```

---

## Step 2: Immediate Support Structure

### Fullback (overlap trigger)

* LB or RB sprints beyond winger

### CM movement

* one arrives edge of box
* one holds rest defense

### ST movement

* attacks between CBs immediately

---

## Step 3: Timing Event

```text id="cr2"
winger → 1 touch / 2 touch cross
```

No delay.

---

## Step 4: Box Movement

* ST → near post sprint
* opposite winger → far post run
* CM → penalty spot arrival
* CBs → chaotic marking recovery

---

## Step 5: Outcome Logic

* defense is still retreating
* space between CBs not organized
* high probability of first-contact goal

---

# 2) DRIVEN CROSS ATTACK (LOW, FAST, PENETRATING BALL)

## Core idea:

A fast, flat cross that travels through defensive line.

---

## Step 1: Setup

```text id="cr3"
winger isolates fullback
```

---

## Step 2: Movement Structure

### ST

* stays central but slightly ahead of CBs

### CM

* attacks edge of box

### opposite winger

* far post sprint

---

## Step 3: Cross Execution

```text id="cr4"
ball driven hard across 6-yard box
```

---

## Step 4: Player Reactions

### ST

* near-post dart OR screen CB

### RW/LW opposite side

* far post tap-in

### CM

* penalty spot rebound zone

---

## Step 5: Key Effect

* ball travels faster than defensive shift
* forces own goals, deflections, rebounds

---

# 3) LOFTED CROSS ATTACK (HIGH ARC DELIVERY)

## Core idea:

Ball is lifted over defensive line into aerial duel zone.

---

## Step 1: Build-up

Wide overload created:

```text id="cr5"
LB + LW + LCM triangle
```

---

## Step 2: Movement

### ST

* central aerial target

### CBs

* forced into jump duel

### CM

* arrives for second ball

---

## Step 3: Cross type

```text id="cr6"
high, slow arc into penalty area
```

---

## Step 4: Box occupation

* ST → central aerial contest
* CBs → marking duel
* opposite winger → far-post header
* CM → rebound zone

---

## Step 5: Outcome

* headers
* knockdowns
* second-ball shots

---

# 4) INSWINGING CROSS ATTACK (BALL CURVES TOWARD GOAL)

## Core idea:

Cross curves **toward goalkeeper and goal line**, increasing danger.

---

## Step 1: Setup

Typically from opposite-footed winger:

```text id="cr7"
RW (left foot) OR LW (right foot)
```

---

## Step 2: Movement

### ST

* near-post aggressive run

### CBs

* forced to face own goal

### GK

* must decide: catch or punch under pressure

---

## Step 3: Cross trajectory

```text id="cr8"
ball curves inward toward goalmouth
```

---

## Step 4: Box reactions

* near-post flicks
* own-goal risk increases
* deflections common

---

## Step 5: Supporting structure

* CM attacks second phase
* opposite winger far-post finish

---

# 5) OUTSWINGING CROSS ATTACK (BALL CURVES AWAY FROM GOAL)

## Core idea:

Ball moves away from goalkeeper into open space.

---

## Step 1: Setup

```text id="cr9"
dominant-foot winger
```

---

## Step 2: Movement structure

### ST

* central run but slightly delayed

### CM

* penalty spot arrival

### RW/LW opposite side

* far-post sprint

---

## Step 3: Cross trajectory

```text id="cr10"
ball curves away from goal into safe aerial zone
```

---

## Step 4: Key advantage

* easier for attacker headers
* goalkeeper cannot intercept easily
* defensive line stretched horizontally

---

## Step 5: Outcome

* clean headers
* controlled finishes
* volley chances at edge of box

---

# 6) CUT-BACK CROSS ATTACK (HIGHEST xG CROSS)

## Core idea:

Ball is played backwards from byline into penalty spot zone.

---

## Step 1: Wide penetration

```text id="cr11"
LW or RB reaches byline
```

---

## Step 2: Box occupation structure

```text id="cr12"
ST → near post
CM → penalty spot
RW/LW → far post
DM → edge of box
```

---

## Step 3: Movement trigger

* winger does NOT cross early
* waits for defenders to collapse

---

## Step 4: Cutback execution

```text id="cr13"
byline → pull back to penalty spot
```

---

## Step 5: Key attacking advantage

* shooter is facing goal
* defense is running toward goal line (wrong direction)

---

## Step 6: Finishing zones

* penalty spot shot (primary)
* edge of box volley
* near-post tap-in

---

# PLAYER ROLE SUMMARY (CROSSING SYSTEMS)

---

## Striker (ST)

* near-post attacker
* aerial duel target
* screens goalkeeper
* primary finisher in crosses

---

## Wingers (LW/RW)

* cross delivery specialists
* byline attackers
* isolation creators
* cutback decision makers

---

## Central Midfielders (LCM/RCM)

* penalty spot attackers
* second-ball collectors
* edge-of-box shooters
* timing runners

---

## Defensive Midfielder (DM)

* holds outside box structure
* prevents counterattack
* arrives for rebound shots

---

## Fullbacks (LB/RB)

* overlap creators
* primary crossing sources
* underlap support in cutbacks

---

## Center-Backs (CBs)

* aerial defenders and attackers
* step into box during set-piece-like crosses

---

# CORE PRINCIPLE OF CROSSING ATTACKS

Crossing systems follow:

```text id="cr14"
WIDE PENETRATION → BOX OCCUPATION → TIMED DELIVERY → MULTI-LAYERED FINISHING
```

Key ideas:

* movement before cross is more important than cross itself
* box occupation determines success rate
* different cross types change attacker timing
* second-ball structure is essential

---

# FINAL SUMMARY

Crossing is not one tactic—it is a **complete attacking subsystem** with different delivery mechanics:

* Early cross → speed before organization
* Driven cross → penetration through chaos
* Lofted cross → aerial contest system
* Inswinging cross → goalkeeper pressure + deflections
* Outswinging cross → controlled aerial finishing
* Cut-back → highest probability structured chance
