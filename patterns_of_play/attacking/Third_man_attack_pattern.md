# Attack Form: Third-Man Run Attack (4-3-3)

Third-man run attacking is one of the most important principles in elite football. It is not a “pattern” in the simple sense—it is a **mechanism that powers almost every other attacking form**.

Core idea:

> The player who receives the final decisive pass is NOT the player who started the combination, and NOT the player who made the second pass—but a *third player arriving into space at the perfect moment*.

It breaks man-marking, bypasses pressure, and creates **unguarded runs into space**.

I’ll break it into key patterns and describe **all 11 players’ movements step-by-step**.

---

# Pattern 1: Classic Third-Man Wall Combination

## Situation

Ball in midfield under pressure.

```text id="tm1"
      ST
LW           RW

   LCM   RCM
       DM

LB  LCB RCB  RB
```

---

## Step 1: First Pass (Player A)

### Example

```text id="tm2"
DM → LCM
```

### Movement

* DM passes and stays
* LCM drops into pocket between lines

---

## Step 2: Second Pass (Player B)

### LCM immediately lays off:

```text id="tm3"
LCM → ST
```

### Movement

* LCM does NOT turn
* One-touch or two-touch pass
* Pulls defender slightly forward

---

## Step 3: Third-Man Run (Player C = RW or LW)

### Key event:

```text id="tm4"
RW → diagonal run behind defense
```

### Why this works:

* ST receives attention from CB
* LCM attracts midfield marker
* RW is completely free (blindside run)

---

## Step 4: Final Action

* ST slips pass into RW run
* RW shoots or crosses

---

## Final Structure

```text id="tm5"
DM → LCM → ST → RW (goal action)
```

# Player Role Summary (Third-Man Run Attack)

---

## Striker (ST)

* Wall player (bounce passes)
* Decoy runner (pins CBs)
* Assist provider for third-man runners
* Key link between midfield and attack

---

## Central Midfielders (LCM/RCM)

* Core facilitators of combinations
* One-touch pass specialists
* Create angles for third-man release
* Late runners into box

---

## Defensive Midfielder (DM)

* Starting point of combinations
* Tempo controller
* Switch initiator
* Pressure absorber before release

---

## Wingers (LW/RW)

* Primary third-man runners
* Blindside attackers
* Finishers from diagonal runs
* Space exploiters after combination

---

## Fullbacks (LB/RB)

* Provide wide depth
* Overlap or underlap after combination
* Support triangle structure

---

## Center-Backs (CBs)

* Initiate vertical third-man chains
* Step into midfield to create passing angles

---

# Core Principle of Third-Man Run Attack

Third-man attacking is built on:

```text id="tm25"
PASS → BOUNCE → THIRD PLAYER EXPLODES INTO SPACE
```

Key ideas:

* The final runner is unmarked because defenders follow the ball
* Timing is more important than speed
* The structure creates “invisible movement lanes”
