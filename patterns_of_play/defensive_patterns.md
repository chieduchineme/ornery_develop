In football (soccer), defending can take many forms depending on the team's height, compactness, pressing triggers, and game state.

### 1. High Press

* Defend by pressing the opponent near their own goal.
* Aims to force rushed passes, turnovers, and attacks from short distances.

### 2. Mid-Block

* Defend from the middle third with compact distances.
* Allows the opponent's first pass, then blocks central progression.

### 3. Low Block

* Defend deep near the penalty area.
* Protects the box, central lanes, and space behind the defensive line.

### 4. Counter-Press

* Press immediately after losing the ball.
* Stops counters before they start and can recover possession high.

### 5. Man-Oriented Pressing

* Defenders follow direct opponents tightly.
* Useful for denying easy short passing options.

### 6. Zonal Defending

* Players protect zones rather than chasing opponents.
* Prioritizes team shape, spacing, and cover.

### 7. Hybrid Defending

* Combines zonal structure with man-oriented pressure near the ball.
* Common in elite teams because it balances control and aggression.

### 8. Compactness Defending

* Team distances shrink vertically and horizontally.
* Makes central progression difficult and forces play outside.

### 9. Pressing Traps

* Team intentionally leaves a passing option open.
* Once the ball enters that area, multiple players collapse around it.

### 10. Touchline Press

* Uses the sideline as an extra defender.
* Pressing angles force the opponent into wide, low-option areas.

### 11. Rest Defense

* Defensive structure maintained while attacking.
* Prevents counter-attacks by keeping cover behind the ball.

### 12. Transition Defense

* Immediate defensive reaction after possession is lost.
* Includes counter-pressing, tactical delay, or dropping into shape.

### 13. Defensive Overload

* Creates numerical superiority around the ball.
* Used to suffocate dangerous zones and win second balls.

### 14. Cover Shadow Defending

* Player presses one opponent while blocking the pass into another.
* Allows one defender to control two options at once.

### 15. Back Five Defending

* Uses five players across the defensive line.
* Protects the width of the box and supports wide center-backs.

### 16. Box Defending

* Prioritizes defending the penalty area.
* Focuses on crosses, cutbacks, rebounds, and second balls.

### 17. Set-Piece Defense

* Defending corners, free kicks, throw-ins, and penalties.
* Can be zonal, man-marking, or hybrid.

### 18. Offside Trap

* Defensive line steps up together to catch attackers offside.
* Requires timing, coordination, and pressure on the passer.

### 19. Funnel Defending

* Team shape guides the opponent toward less dangerous zones.
* Often forces play wide, backward, or onto weaker-foot players.

### 20. Goalkeeper Sweeper Defense

* Goalkeeper defends space behind a high line.
* Supports aggressive pressing and offside-based defending.

Most modern teams combine several of these rather than using just one. For example, Manchester City often mixes counter-pressing, rest defense, high pressing, and goalkeeper sweeping, while Atletico Madrid have been known for compact mid-blocks, low blocks, box defending, and transition control.
